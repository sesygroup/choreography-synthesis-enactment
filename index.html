Highly-collaborative distributed systems: synthesis and enactment at work

This web page contains the replication package of the paper we submitted to the IEEE Transactions on Services Computing with title Highly-collaborative distributed systems: synthesis and enactment at work.

Abstract

The Future Internet is populated by a virtually infinite number of software services. This situation promotes a reuse-based software production through the composition and coordination of existing software services in a way that the specified requirements are met. Service choreographies support a distributed composition approach that is based on the specification of the external interaction of the participant services in terms of flows of message exchanges, given from a global perspective. When third-party services are to be composed, obtaining the coordination logic required to realize the specified choreography is a non-trivial and error-prone task. Automatic support is then needed. In this paper, we present an integrated development and run-time environment for choreography-based systems, which covers all the development activities, including specification, code synthesis, automatic deployment, enactment, and monitoring on the Cloud. We report the results of an experiment that we conducted with a use case in the in-store marketing and sales domain. Results confirm confidence in the approach, and show that the platform can be applied in practical contexts. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to bring the adoption of choreographies to the development practices currently adopted by IT companies.

Contributors

Marco Autili

is assistant professor - University of L'Aquila's Department of Information Engineering, Computer Science, and Mathematics. His research focuses on automated synthesis for composing distributed systems, formal specification and checking of temporal properties, context-oriented programming, and resource-oriented analysis of adaptable (mobile) applications. Autili received a PhD in computer science from the University of L'Aquila.

Fabio Kon

is full professor - University of São Paulo, Computer Science. His research focuses on Distributed Systems, Smart Cities, Agile Methods, Open Source, and Entrepreneurship. Prof. Kon is an ACM Distinguished Scientist and a vibraphonist.

Alexander Perucci

is postdoctoral researcher - University of L'Aquila's Department of Information Engineering, Computer Science, and Mathematics. His research focuses on automatic synthesis and composition of software artifacts to build Choreography-Based Service- IoT-Oriented Systems. Perucci received a PhD in computer science from the University of L'Aquila.
